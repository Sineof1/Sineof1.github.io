Principia, Problem 3, Conclusion

Here's Part 1 and Part 2. We'll recap in a sec, but let's get this Lemma 1 out of the way. The image for this comes from the excellent book on which all of these notes are based, called The Key to Newton's Dynamics.

In Lemma 1, Newton shows that the area of Parallelogram A, \(\mathtt{2 \cdot m\overline{AC} \times 2 \cdot m\overline{BC} = 4(AC)(BC)}\) is equal to the area of Parallelogram B, \(\mathtt{2 \cdot m\overline{CD} \times 2 \cdot m\overline{PF} = 4(CD)(PF)}\). This means that \(\color{blue}{\mathtt{\frac{CA}{PF} = \frac{CD}{CB}}}\).

Now let's recall the table of ratios and congruences we had worked out so far.

\(\small\mathtt{\overline{EP} \cong \overline{AC}}\)\(\mathtt{\frac{L \cdot QR}{L \cdot PV} = \frac{AC}{PC}}\)
\(\mathtt{\frac{GV \cdot VP}{QV^2} = \frac{(PC)^2}{(DC)^2}}\)\(\mathtt{\frac{(QX)^2}{(QT)^2} = \frac{(EP)^2}{(PF)^2}}\)
\(\mathtt{\frac{L \cdot PV}{GV \cdot VP} = \frac{L}{GV}}\)\(\mathtt{\frac{(QV)^2}{(QX)^2} = \frac{M}{N}}\)
\(\small\mathtt{\overline{EP} \cong \overline{AC}}\)\(\mathtt{\frac{L \cdot QR}{L \cdot PV} = \frac{AC}{PC}}\)
\(\mathtt{\frac{GV \cdot VP}{QV^2} = \frac{(PC)^2}{(DC)^2}}\)\(\mathtt{\frac{(QX)^2}{(QT)^2} = \frac{(CD)^2}{(CB)^2}}\)
\(\mathtt{\frac{L \cdot PV}{GV \cdot VP} = \frac{L}{GV}}\)\(\mathtt{\frac{(QV)^2}{(QX)^2} = \frac{M}{N}}\)

Since \(\mathtt{\overline{EP} \cong \overline{AC}}\), substituting EP for CA in the blue ratio above gives us \(\mathtt{\frac{EP}{PF} = \frac{CD}{CB}}\). But according to the 2nd column, 2nd row of the table, these two ratios are also equal to \(\mathtt{\frac{QX}{QT}}\).

Thus, we can write \(\mathtt{\frac{(QX)^2}{(QT)^2} = \frac{(CD)^2}{(CB)^2}}\). And we'll replace that 2nd row, 2nd column proportion with this new one, as the final domino to be set up before multiplying and simplifying.

We're inches away.


Knock 'Em Down

We have 5 equivalent ratios above. We can multiply all the left-hand sides and set the product equal to the product of all the right-hand sides: \[\mathtt{\frac{(L \cdot QR) \times (GV \cdot VP) \times (QX)^2 \times (L \cdot PV) \times (QV)^2}{(L \cdot PV) \times (QV)^2 \times (QT)^2 \times (GV \cdot VP) \times (QX)^2} = \frac{AC \times (PC)^2 \times (CD)^2 \times L \times M}{PC \times (DC)^2 \times (CB)^2 \times GV \times N}}\]

The left-hand side simplifies nicely to \(\mathtt{\frac{L \cdot QR}{(QT)^2}}\). For the right-hand side, immediately we have only \(\mathtt{(CD)^2}\) and \(\mathtt{(DC)^2}\) canceling, and the PC terms simplifying. But this looks nicer already: \[\mathtt{\frac{L \cdot QR}{(QT)^2} = \frac{AC \times PC \times L \times M}{(CB)^2 \times GV \times N}}\]

The latus rectum, \(\mathtt{L}\), which we looked at last time, has a constant length of \(\mathtt{\frac{2 \cdot (BC)^2}{AC}}\), which means that \(\mathtt{L \times AC = (BC)^2}\).

So, we can simplify even further: \[\mathtt{\frac{L \cdot QR}{(QT)^2} = \frac{2 \times PC \times M}{GV \times N} = \frac{2(PC)}{GV} \times \frac{M}{N}}\]

Finally, the Limit

Newton's mathematical analysis wants to highlight what happens right at point P (the planet). The line segment \(\mathtt{QR}\) is one part of the force that Newton is trying to describe. Instead of barreling off on the tangent \(\mathtt{\overrightarrow{PR}}\), as it is inclined to do (by Newton's first assumption, or first law, whatever), the planet at point P deviates from that tangential path due to a mysterious force thought to emanate from the Sun. The strength of this force is something that Newton knows from Galileo: the force is proportional to the displacement \(\mathtt{QR}\) divided by the time squared. The time is equal to the area of \(\mathtt{\Delta SPQ}\) highlighted in yellow below (I'll come back to show this in a later post). Because this is a proportional relationship, we can ignore the one-half for the area of SPQ, and conclude that the force is proportional to \(\mathtt{\frac{QR}{(SP)^2 \cdot (QT)^2}}\).

But \(\mathtt{QR}\) is the deviation after a bit of time, not right when the planet is at point \(\mathtt{P}\). The point \(\mathtt{Q}\) is actually the planet at point \(\mathtt{P}\) after it has spent time traveling more around its orbit. So, to know what happens at point \(\mathtt{P}\), we have to inch point \(\mathtt{Q}\) back along the ellipse toward point \(\mathtt{P}\). What happens to all our mathematical work when we do this? How does our diagram change?

Try to imagine it on the diagram. \(\mathtt{QQ'}\) gets shorter and moves closer to point \(\mathtt{P}\). Same goes for the teeny tiny line segment \(\mathtt{XV}\).

Newton saw that as Q approaches P, the line segment \(\mathtt{GV}\) becomes equal to \(\mathtt{PG}\), which is equal to \(\mathtt{2 \cdot PC}\) (remember that?). Thus, the ratio above, \(\mathtt{\frac{2(PC)}{GV}}\), becomes 1. The same goes for \(\mathtt{\frac{M}{N}}\), which you might remember (it's in the table above) is equal to \(\mathtt{\frac{(QV)^2}{(QX)^2}}\). This ratio goes to 1 as well when point Q approaches infinitesimally close to point P. So, our equation becomes: \[\mathtt{\frac{L \cdot QR}{(QT)^2} = 1 \rightarrow \frac{QR}{(QT)^2} = \frac{1}{L}}\]

If we multiply that second equation through by \(\mathtt{\frac{1}{(SP)^2}}\), we get \[\mathtt{\frac{QR}{(SP)^2 \cdot (QT)^2} = \frac{1}{L \cdot (SP)^2}}\]

And we know from Galileo above that the left side of that equation is proportional to the force, or \(\mathtt{F \propto \frac{QR}{(SP)^2 \cdot (QT)^2}}\). So now we know that it is proportional to the right side as well. This means that \(\mathtt{F \propto \frac{1}{L \cdot (SP)^2}}\). And because \(\mathtt{L}\) is a constant, we can throw it out. Which leaves us with the grand finale, that the force is proportional to the inverse square of the distance from the Sun to the planet: \[\mathtt{F \propto \frac{1}{(SP)^2}}\]
