Connecting the Operations

The graph below shows one way we can think about "connecting" the four basic operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division on the coordinate plane. We graph linear equations of the form \(\mathtt{y = x \circ p}\), where \(\mathtt{x, p \in}\) \(\mathbb{R}\) and the \(\mathtt{\circ}\) represents any of the four operations mentioned.

Four points of intersection are also graphed. Press the play button in the expressions list to see some nifty properties of this setup. I'll add more to this post in the future, but I'm always curious to know what other folks see with these kinds of things too. Leave your observations in the comments!

Observations

Most interesting to look at for me is the symmetry of the coordinates for the intersections. I've got multiplication and addition connected at (\(\mathtt{\color{red}{\frac{p}{p - 1}, \frac{p^2}{p - 1}}}\)). Reverse the \(\mathtt{x}\) and \(\mathtt{y}\) (reflect over an axis formed by \(\mathtt{y = x}\)), and those coordinates now connect division and subtraction: (\(\mathtt{\color{green}{\frac{p^2}{p - 1}, \frac{p}{p - 1}}}\)). Take the red intersection and reflect over the \(\mathtt{x}\)- and \(\mathtt{y}\)-axes by making both coordinates negative, and we've connected multiplication and subtraction at (\(\mathtt{\color{blue}{\frac{-p}{p - 1}, \frac{-(p^2)}{p - 1}}}\)). Then, finally, flipping the blue connection over a \(\mathtt{y = x}\) axis gives us a point that connects division and addition with (\(\mathtt{\color{purple}{\frac{-(p^2)}{p - 1}, \frac{-p}{p - 1}}}\)).
